Swami Vivekananda

1863 - 1902

"Arise, awake, and stop not until the goal is achieved."

Biography

Swami Vivekananda, born Narendranath Datta, was an Indian Hindu monk and a key figure in the introduction of Indian philosophies of Vedanta and Yoga to the Western world. He was a major force in the revival of Hinduism in India and contributed to the concept of nationalism in colonial India. He is best known for his speech which began with the words - "Sisters and brothers of America ..." at the Parliament of the World's Religions in Chicago in 1893.

Vivekananda was born into an aristocratic Bengali family in Calcutta and was inclined towards spirituality. He was a disciple of the Indian mystic Ramakrishna. After the death of Ramakrishna, Vivekananda toured the Indian subcontinent extensively and acquired first-hand knowledge of the conditions prevailing in British India. Later, he travelled to the United States, representing India at the 1893 Parliament of the World's Religions.

He conducted hundreds of public and private lectures and classes, disseminating tenets of Hindu philosophy in the United States, England, and Europe. In India, Vivekananda is regarded as a patriotic saint and his birthday is celebrated as National Youth Day.

Famous Quotes

"The greatest religion is to be true to your own nature. Have faith in yourselves!"

"In a conflict between the heart and the brain, follow your heart."

Achievements

  • Represented India at the 1893 Parliament of the World's Religions.
  • Founded the Ramakrishna Mission in 1897.
  • Played a significant role in the revival of modern Hinduism.
  • Instrumental in introducing the philosophies of Yoga and Vedanta to the Western world.
